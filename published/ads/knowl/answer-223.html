Answer 11.5.5.7.1.

Assume that \(H\) and \(K\) are subgroups of group \(G\text{,}\) and that, as in FigureÂ 11.5.12, there are elements \(x \in H - K\) and \(y \in K - H\text{.}\) Consider the product \(x * y\text{.}\) Where could it be placed in the Venn diagram? If we can prove that it must lie in the outer region, \(H^c \cap K^c=(H \cup K)^c\text{,}\) then we have proven that \(H \cup K\) is not closed under \(*\) and cannot be a subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) Assume that \(x*y\in H\text{.}\) Since \(x\) is in \(H\text{,}\) \(x^{-1}\) is in \(H\) and so by closure \(x^{-1}*(x * y )= y \in H\) which is a contradiction. Similarly, \(x*y \notin K\text{.}\)
One way to interpret this theorem is that no group is the union of two groups.
